Sediment Transport

Understanding hydraulic sorting, downstream fining, and debunking the "Maturity Myth" in sedimentary petrology

2. Hydraulic Sorting

Hydrodynamic processes can drastically modify sediment mineralogy locally through the principle of hydraulic equivalence:

üîë Hydraulic Equivalence Principle

Grains with the same settling velocity are deposited together. Because settling velocity depends on submerged weight, grains of a given size and density settle as fast as smaller grains of higher density. Result: heavy minerals are always finer-grained than associated light minerals in sorted sediments.

Effects on Composition

The Nile example demonstrates this dramatically: the same sediment plots in different QFL provenance fields depending entirely on grain size‚Äîsilty levees plot as "magmatic arc," fine bar sands as "recycled orogen," and medium bar sands as "continental block."

4. The "Maturity Myth" ‚Äî A Critical Evaluation

‚ö†Ô∏è The Myth

The traditional belief that sediments progressively increase their "maturity" through time‚Äîbecoming more rounded, better sorted, and more quartz-rich‚Äîas if their destiny were to reach a final stage of perfection represented by pure quartz spheres.

Reality Check: Rounding

Russell and Taylor (1937) studying Mississippi River sands found: "detrital grains show no evidence of becoming rounded as a result of attrition during transportation. On the contrary, larger sand grains become slightly more angular downstream." Key findings:

  • Rounding occurs rapidly in air (eolian) transport but extremely slowly in water
  • Stream sands usually remain angular or become more angular
  • The concept of "rivers rounding their sands" is an armchair theory without observational support

Reality Check: Sorting

Sorting is achieved quasi-instantaneously at the moment of deposition‚Äîit has little to do with cumulative "modifying energy" through time:

  • Tractive currents: Well-sorted deposits (instant sorting during settling)
  • Gravity-dominated: Poor sorting (debris flows, till)
  • There is no sequential progression from poor to good sorting through time

Reality Check: Mineralogical Maturity

Mechanical wear is unable to modify sand composition markedly. Major exceptions:

  • Shale/siltstone fragments: Destroyed rapidly at fluvial-beach transition
  • Carbonate grains in eolian environments: Round faster, decrease in size, may be lost
  • Cleavable amphibole: Splits into platy grains in littoral environments

üîë The Real Source of "Maturity"

If recycled sediments are quartz-rich with ZTR-dominated heavy mineral suites, it's because their parent sandstones underwent diagenetic dissolution for millions of years at elevated temperatures before erosion‚ÄîNOT because of mechanical transport processes.

5. Recycling and the Persistence of Unstable Grains

A striking counterexample to the maturity myth: the Aqsu River (Iraq) drains only Cenozoic strata of the Zagros foothills, yet its sand is:

  • Dominated by unstable grains: mafic volcanic/metavolcanic fragments, epidote, pyroxene
  • Quartz represents only 5% of the sediment
  • ZTR index is only 2

This demonstrates that recycling (a physical process) in a weathering-limited regime tends to reproduce the mineralogy of parent clastic rocks, not increase maturity. Sand recycled from orogenic flysch may contain <10% quartz.

üìå Key Points Summary

  • Clay = suspended (chemical weathering); Sand/Gravel = bedload (physical erosion)
  • Hydraulic equivalence: same settling velocity ‚Üí deposited together; heavy minerals are finer
  • Grain-size sorting alone can shift apparent QFL provenance across all fields
  • Downstream fining controlled by selective deposition + subsidence, not abrasion
  • Gravel-sand transition (1‚Äì5 mm) shows accelerated fining rates
  • The "Maturity Myth" is NOT supported by observations
  • Rounding occurs rapidly in air, slowly in water‚Äîrivers don't round sands
  • Sorting is instantaneous at deposition, not cumulative through time
  • Mechanical transport barely modifies mineralogy‚Äîdiagenesis creates "maturity"
  • Recycled sediments can be quartz-poor if parent rocks were quartz-poor
