Diagenesis

Understanding how burial processes modify sediment composition and affect provenance signal preservation

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand the main phases of sandstone diagenesis (eogenesis, mesogenesis, telogenesis)
  • Identify the four main diagenetic processes: compaction, dissolution, cementation, replacement
  • Recognize how diagenesis affects framework grains and heavy minerals
  • Understand the controls on diagenetic intensity and provenance signal preservation
  • Explain the relationship between original composition and diagenetic pathways
  • Apply diagenetic concepts to provenance interpretation in ancient sandstones

1. Diagenetic Phases and Environments

Diagenesis encompasses all physical, chemical, and biological changes affecting sediments after deposition and before metamorphism. Three main phases are recognized:

PhaseEnvironmentKey Processes
EogenesisShallow burial, under influence of depositional environmentMechanical compaction, early cementation, interaction with pore waters
MesogenesisEffective burial, elevated T and PChemical compaction, dissolution, clay/quartz cements, destruction of primary porosity
TelogenesisUplift, interaction with meteoric waterSecondary dissolution, oxidation, karstification

üîë Time-Temperature Relationship

The time and temperature available for chemical reactions during burial are incomparably longer and higher than during sediment transit from source to sink. This is why diagenesis‚Äînot transport‚Äîis the primary driver of "compositional maturation."

2. Main Diagenetic Processes

Compaction

Mechanical compaction (early burial) reduces porosity through grain rearrangement and deformation of ductile grains. Chemical compaction (deeper burial) involves pressure solution at grain contacts, creating sutured boundaries and generating silica for cement precipitation.

Ductile grains are particularly susceptible:

  • Volcanic lithics: Deform and create pseudomatrix
  • Carbonate grains: Pressure-dissolve easily
  • Low-grade metamorphic lithics: Squeeze between rigid grains

Dissolution

Dissolution creates secondary porosity but also destroys provenance information:

Mineral/GrainSusceptibilityProducts/Effects
FeldsparsHighKaolinite, sericite, calcite replacement; secondary porosity
Volcanic lithicsHighChloritization, zeolitization
Carbonate lithicsVariableComplete dissolution or dolomitization
Garnet, amphibole, pyroxeneHigh in acidic fluidsComplete removal possible
Zircon, tourmaline, rutileVery lowNearly immune to dissolution

Cementation

Main cement types and their distribution:

  • Early carbonate cement: Can preserve original texture and porosity if occluding pores early
  • Quartz overgrowths: Common in quartzose sandstones; sourced from pressure solution
  • Clay cements: Kaolinite (eogenesis), illite/chlorite (mesogenesis)
  • Clay transformation: Illite-smectite ‚Üí kaolinite ‚Üí illite/chlorite with depth

Replacement

Minerals can be replaced while retaining original grain shape (pseudomorphs), complicating provenance interpretation but preserving some information.

The type and extent of diagenetic processes depend on four main factors (Caracciolo, 2020):

Fluid compositionAcidic fluids ‚Üí feldspar dissolution; Alkaline fluids ‚Üí carbonate cementation
Temperature √ó TimeHigher T and longer exposure ‚Üí more complete reactions

üîë Preserving Provenance Signals

Early diagenesis (e.g., carbonate cements from rapid evaporation) can preserve pristine provenance signatures by occluding pore space and inhibiting further compaction, cementation, and dissolution during subsequent burial‚Äîunless alkaline fluids later dissolve these early cements.

4. Diagenesis and Heavy Mineral Assemblages

Heavy minerals are particularly affected by diagenetic dissolution, creating the characteristic increase in ZTR index (zircon + tourmaline + rutile) with stratigraphic age:

Garzanti and And√≤ (2007) showed that the ZTR index increases systematically with stratigraphic age‚Äînot because of transport-related "maturation," but because of diagenetic dissolution during burial. This is a major bias in provenance analysis of ancient sandstones.

Stability Series (Morton and Hallsworth, 2007)

From most to least stable during burial diagenesis:

  1. Zircon, tourmaline, rutile (ultra-stable)
  2. Apatite, monazite, titanite (moderately stable)
  3. Garnet, staurolite, kyanite (variably stable)
  4. Amphibole, pyroxene, epidote (unstable)
  5. Olivine (highly unstable)

Fortunately, the most common minerals used in detrital geochronology (zircon, apatite, rutile, monazite, titanite) are nearly immune to diagenesis, making U-Pb dating robust even in deeply buried sandstones.

5. Composition-Dependent Diagenetic Pathways

Original sand composition strongly controls which diagenetic pathways develop:

Original CompositionDominant DiagenesisEffect on Provenance
Quartzose (Q-rich)Quartz cementation, pressure solutionWell preserved; porosity reduced
Feldspathic (F-rich)Feldspar dissolution ‚Üí secondary porosity; albitizationModerate loss of feldspar signal
Lithic (L-rich)Compaction ‚Üí pseudomatrix; dissolution of volcanic/carbonate lithicsSevere loss; apparent shift toward Q-rich

üîë The "Diagenetic Maturation" Effect

If recycled sediments appear quartz-rich with ZTR-dominated heavy mineral suites, it's primarily because their parent sandstones underwent prolonged diagenetic dissolution at elevated temperatures (many millions of years, tens of degrees) BEFORE exhumation and erosion‚Äînot because of mechanical transport processes.

6. Recognizing and Correcting for Diagenesis

Diagnostic Features

  • Oversized pores: Secondary porosity from dissolved feldspars
  • Pseudomatrix: Squeezed ductile grains (volcanic, metamorphic)
  • Skeletal grains: Deeply etched feldspars, corroded heavy minerals
  • Authigenic minerals: Kaolinite booklets, illite fibers, quartz overgrowths
  • High ZTR index: In rocks older than Neogene, suspect diagenetic culling

Best Practices

  • Document diagenetic features systematically during point counting
  • Identify relict grain outlines even when partially dissolved
  • Use multiple provenance proxies (petrography + geochronology + geochemistry)
  • Consider burial history when interpreting heavy mineral assemblages
  • Favor diagenesis-resistant proxies (zircon U-Pb) for deep-time studies

üìå Key Points Summary

  • Three diagenetic phases: eogenesis (shallow), mesogenesis (burial), telogenesis (uplift)
  • Four main processes: compaction, dissolution, cementation, replacement
  • Diagenesis‚Äînot transport‚Äîcreates "compositional maturation"
  • Feldspars and unstable lithics are highly susceptible to dissolution
  • ZTR index increases with stratigraphic age due to diagenetic dissolution
  • Early cementation can preserve pristine provenance signatures
  • Original composition controls diagenetic pathways
  • Zircon and other geochronology minerals are nearly immune to diagenesis
  • Always document and correct for diagenetic effects in provenance studies
  • Multi-proxy approaches are essential for ancient sandstones
