1. Weathering: The Foundation of Sediment Generation

Weathering represents the first step in the sedimentary cycle, transforming coherent bedrock into loose detritus that can be transported by surface processes. The interaction of precipitation and temperature determines the kinetics of chemical reactions and ultimately controls the degree of rock alteration (Johnsson, 1993; Caracciolo, 2020).

Two fundamental weathering mechanisms operate in surface environments:

  • Chemical weathering: Dissolution, hydrolysis, and oxidation reactions that alter mineral chemistry and produce secondary minerals (clays, oxides)
  • Mechanical weathering: Physical fragmentation by frost wedging, thermal expansion, root growth, and abrasion during transport

üîë Key Concept: Climate-Lithology Interaction

The mineralogical and textural parameters of lithologies exposed to surface processes are the ultimate controls on sediment production. Different rock types respond differently to the same climatic conditions, and the same rock type weathers differently under different climates.

2. The Sand Generation Index (SGI)

Palomares and Arribas (1993) introduced the Sand Generation Index (SGI) to quantify the capacity of different lithologies to produce sand-sized sediment relative to a reference rock type. This concept recognizes that in any given tectonic and climatic setting, different source rocks contribute disproportionately to the sand fraction.

3. Chemical Weathering and Its Measurement

3.1 The Chemical Index of Alteration (CIA)

The most widely used geochemical proxy for weathering intensity is the Chemical Index of Alteration (Nesbitt & Young, 1982):

The CIA theoretically measures the conversion of feldspars to clay minerals. Fresh granitoids have CIA values around 45‚Äì55, while intensely weathered tropical soils approach 100 (kaolinite = 100).

3.2 Critical Limitations of CIA

Despite its popularity, the CIA suffers from fundamental problems that limit its usefulness in provenance studies:

  • Provenance dominance: In high-relief settings, CIA values primarily reflect source rock composition rather than weathering intensity (Garzanti & Resentini, 2016)
  • Hydraulic sorting bias: Grain-size fractionation affects element distribution‚Äîclays concentrate Al, while sands retain more alkalis
  • Diagenetic modification: K-metasomatism can reset original CIA values; CaO in cements contaminates the signal
  • Mixed sources: CIA cannot distinguish weathered felsic sources from fresh mafic sources, which naturally have different chemistries

üîë Key Insight: Hatzenb√ºhler et al. (2022)

In high-relief tropical drainages of the Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta (Colombia), traditional chemical weathering indices fail to detect weathering signals. Bulk compositions show low CIA values (unweathered region) despite clear petrographic evidence of feldspar alteration. This is typical for dynamic orogenic settings where physical erosion outruns chemical weathering.

3.3 Alternative Approaches

More reliable weathering assessment combines:

  • Petrographic observation: Feldspar alteration stages, corrosion features, sericitization
  • Œ±Al-values: Element-specific weathering indices that track individual element mobility
  • Clay mineralogy: Weathering recorded in the fine fraction (kaolinite, smectite assemblages)
  • Heavy mineral stability indices: ZTR index, ATi (apatite-tourmaline index)

4. Relief: The Master Control

Perhaps the most important insight from modern provenance studies is that relief is the dominant factor controlling whether weathering modifies provenance signals (Garzanti, 2016; Caracciolo, 2020).

4.1 The Relief-Weathering Relationship

Setting Relief Residence Time Weathering Effect
High relief / Orogenic Steep slopes, rapid erosion Short Provenance preserved; chemical weathering minimal
Moderate relief Transitional Intermediate Variable; depends on lithology and climate
Low relief / Cratonic Gentle slopes, slow erosion Long Intense weathering; quartz enrichment; provenance obscured

üîë Key Concept: Physical Erosion Outpaces Chemical Weathering

In high-relief settings, erosion rates exceed weathering rates. Fresh bedrock is continuously exposed and removed before significant chemical alteration can occur. This explains why Himalayan-derived sediments preserve provenance signals despite traveling through monsoon climates where intense chemical weathering would be expected.

4.2 Case Study: Neighboring Drainages with Same Climate

Hatzenb√ºhler et al. (2022) compared the Tapias and Jer√©z rivers in Colombia‚Äîsame lithological assemblage, same climate, but different relief:

  • Jer√©z (steeper relief): Shorter residence time ‚Üí better preserved feldspars, lower weathering stages
  • Tapias (gentler relief): Longer residence time ‚Üí intensive sericitization and corrosion of feldspars

This demonstrates that relief controls weathering intensity independently of climate.

5. Mechanical Weathering and the Maturity Question

5.1 Mechanical Breakdown During Transport

Mechanical weathering during fluvial transport has been traditionally assumed to progressively "mature" sediments by selectively destroying labile minerals. However, modern studies challenge this assumption:

  • Studies on granitoid weathering profiles show Q/F increases of only 2‚Äì20% from bedrock to transported sand (Le Pera et al., 2001; von Eynatten et al., 2016)
  • Most compositional modification occurs during soil formation and grus development, not during transport
  • Extreme mechanical weathering in glacial environments can cause up to 50% feldspar loss (Caracciolo et al., 2012b)

‚ö†Ô∏è The Maturity Myth (Preview)

The traditional concept that transport progressively matures sediments toward quartz-rich compositions is largely incorrect. This "Maturity Myth" will be examined in detail in Module 4 (Transport), where we will see that compositional maturation requires chemical weathering or diagenesis, not physical transport.

5.2 Temporary Storage: A Hidden Weathering Reactor

Sediment temporarily stored in floodplains, terraces, and bars experiences extended residence time in the weathering environment. This can significantly enhance chemical alteration:

  • Floodplain storage exposes sand to soil-forming processes
  • Oxidation, clay infiltration, and dissolution occur during storage
  • Remobilized sediment may be significantly more weathered than first-cycle material

6. Petrographic Recognition of Weathering

Thin-section petrography provides the most direct evidence of weathering intensity in sand samples:

6.1 Feldspar Alteration Stages

Stage Description Petrographic Features
Fresh (1) Unaltered Clear crystals, sharp extinction, intact twinning
Slight (2) Incipient alteration Dusty appearance, minor sericite, corroded edges
Moderate (3) Significant alteration Patchy sericite, dissolution pits, cloudy cores
Strong (4) Advanced alteration Pervasive sericitization, skeletal outlines, corrosion holes
Complete (5) Nearly destroyed Clay pseudomorphs, only remnant grain shapes

6.2 Heavy Mineral Stability

Heavy minerals record weathering through differential dissolution. The classic stability sequence (from unstable to stable):

Olivine < Pyroxene < Amphibole < Epidote < Garnet < Apatite < Titanite < Tourmaline < Rutile < Zircon

The ZTR index (% zircon + tourmaline + rutile among transparent heavy minerals) increases with weathering/recycling intensity.

  • The Sand Generation Index (SGI) quantifies differential sand production from different lithologies
  • Granitoids and gneisses produce the most sand; volcanics and carbonates produce little
  • CIA is widely used but suffers from provenance dominance, sorting bias, and diagenetic overprinting
  • Relief is the master control‚Äîhigh relief preserves provenance signals even in wet climates
  • Physical erosion outpaces chemical weathering in orogenic settings
  • Temporary storage in floodplains extends residence time and enhances weathering
  • Petrographic feldspar alteration stages provide direct evidence of weathering intensity
  • Heavy mineral stability sequences record progressive weathering/diagenetic dissolution
